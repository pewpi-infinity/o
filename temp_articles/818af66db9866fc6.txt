    <title>Resonant charge transfer of hydrogen Rydberg atoms incident at a Cu(100) projected band-gap surface</title>
    <updated>2015-05-06T14:22:06Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1504.07191v3" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1504.07191v3" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>The charge transfer (ionization) of hydrogen Rydberg atoms (principal quantum number $n=25-34$) incident at a Cu(100) surface is investigated. Unlike fully metallic surfaces, where the Rydberg electron energy is degenerate with the conduction band of the metal, the Cu(100) surface has a projected bandgap at these energies, and only discrete image states are available through which charge transfer can take place. Resonant enhancement of charge transfer is observed at hydrogen principal quantum numbers for which the Rydberg energy matches the energy of one of the image states. The integrated surface ionization signals show clear periodicity as the energies of states with increasing $n$ come in and out of resonance with the image states. The velocity dependence of the surface ionization dynamics is also investigated. Decreased velocity of the incident H atom leads to a greater mean distance of ionization and a lower field required to extract the ion. The surface-ionization profiles (signal versus applied field) for `on resonance' $n$ values show a changing shape as the velocity is changed, reflecting the restriction of the resonance to a certain range of applied field.</summary>

    <title>An optically enhanced crystalline silicon allotrope: hydrogen passivated type II silicon clathrate</title>
    <updated>2025-11-12T21:37:45Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.09755v1" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.09755v1" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>While Si clathrates have been explored as promising direct bandgap semiconductors, their practical optoelectronic performance has been limited by high doping levels and structural defects. Hydrogen has long been used to improve the optoelectronic quality of conventional Si, yet its role in clathrate structures remains unexplored. In this study, we demonstrate that hydrogen (deuterium) can be incorporated into type II Si clathrate framework using remote plasma treatment. This process leads to the formation of NaD and SiD complexes, which significantly reduce both the Na donor density and dangling bond defects. Electron paramagnetic resonance confirms nearly a tenfold decrease in Na-related donor states, resulting in the lowest doping level reported in Si clathrates to date. Following passivation, the integrated photoluminescence intensity increases by a factor of 40, accompanied by a blue shift of the main emission peak, consistent with a transition closer to the intrinsic band edge. A new emission peak at 930 nm, attributed to hydrogen-related recombination centers, also appears. These improvements remain stable up to 400 oC. Altogether, this work establishes hydrogen passivation as a viable strategy for enhancing light emission in Si clathrates and opens a new pathway toward their application in Si-based light-emitting diodes and other direct-bandgap optoelectronic devices.</summary>

    <title>Persistent room temperature photodarkening in Cu-doped \b{eta}-Ga2O3</title>
    <updated>2022-01-05T23:12:37Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2201.01861v1" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2201.01861v1" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>Beta-Ga2O3 is an ultra-wide bandgap semiconductor with emerging applications in power electronics. The introduction of acceptor dopants yields semi-insulating substrates necessary for thin-film devices. In the present work, exposure of Cu-doped Ga2O3 to UV light &gt; 4 eV is shown to cause large, persistent photo-induced darkening at room temperature. Electron paramagnetic resonance spectroscopy indicates that light exposure converts Cu2+ to Cu3+, a rare oxidation state that is responsible for the optical absorption. The photodarkening is accompanied by the appearance of O-H vibrational modes in the infrared spectrum. Hybrid function calculations show that Cu acceptors can favorably complex with hydrogen donors incorporated as interstitial (Hi) or substitutional (H_O) defects. When Cu_Ga-H_O complexes absorb light, hydrogen is released, contributing to the observed Cu3+ species and O-H modes.</summary>
