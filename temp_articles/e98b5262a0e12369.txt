    <title>Tetraquark resonances computed with static lattice QCD potentials and scattering theory</title>
    <updated>2017-11-23T21:18:32Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.08830v1" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1711.08830v1" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>We study tetraquark resonances with lattice QCD potentials computed for two static quarks and two dynamical quarks, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation and the emergent wave method of scattering theory. As a proof of concept we focus on systems with isospin $I = 0$, but consider different relative angular momenta $l$ of the heavy $b$ quarks. We compute the phase shifts and search for $\mbox{S}$ and $\mbox{T}$ matrix poles in the second Riemann sheet. We predict a new tetraquark resonance for $l = 1$, decaying into two $B$ mesons, with quantum numbers $I(J^P) = 0(1^-)$, mass $m = 10576_{-4}^{+4} \, \textrm{MeV}$ and decay width $Γ= 112_{-103}^{+90} \, \textrm{MeV}$.</summary>

    <title>Quantum computation toolbox for decoherence-free qubits using multi-band alkali atoms</title>
    <updated>2019-11-05T17:20:53Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1911.01949v1" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1911.01949v1" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>We introduce protocols for designing and manipulating qubits with ultracold alkali atoms in 3D optical lattices. These qubits are formed from two-atom spin superposition states that create a decoherence-free subspace immune to stray magnetic fields, dramatically improving coherence times while still enjoying the single-site addressability and Feshbach resonance control of state-of-the-art alkali atom systems. Our protocol requires no continuous driving or spin-dependent potentials, and instead relies upon the population of a higher motional band to realize naturally tunable in-site exchange and cross-site superexchange interactions. As a proof-of-principle example of their utility for entanglement generation for quantum computation, we show the cross-site superexchange interactions can be used to engineer 1D cluster states. Explicit protocols for experimental preparation and manipulation of the qubits are also discussed, as well as methods for measuring more complex quantities such as out-of-time-ordered correlation functions (OTOCs).</summary>
