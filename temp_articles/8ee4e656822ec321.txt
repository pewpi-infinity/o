    <title>All-atomic generation and noise-quadrature filtering of squeezed vacuum in hot Rb vapor</title>
    <updated>2012-08-02T20:27:17Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/1204.3967v2" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/1204.3967v2" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>With our all-atomic squeezing and filtering setup, we demonstrate control over the noise amplitudes and manipulation of the frequency-dependent squeezing angle of a squeezed vacuum quantum state by passing it through an atomic medium with electromagnetically induced transparency (EIT). We generate low sideband frequency squeezed vacuum using the polarization self-rotation effect in a hot Rb vapor cell, and input it into a second atomic vapor subject to EIT conditions. We use the frequency-dependent absorption of the EIT window to demonstrate an example of squeeze amplitude attenuation and squeeze angle rotation of the quantum noise quadratures of the squeezed probe. These studies have implications for quantum memory and storage as well as gravitational wave interferometric detectors.</summary>

    <title>Vacuum squeezing enhanced micrometer scale vapor cell magnetometer</title>
    <updated>2025-07-10T11:53:01Z</updated>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.07672v1" rel="alternate" type="text/html"/>
    <link href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.07672v1" rel="related" type="application/pdf" title="pdf"/>
    <summary>We report on an optical magnetometer enhanced by vacuum-squeezed light, employing an Mx magnetometer based on $^{87}$Rb vapor in a micrometer-scale cell (~100 $Î¼$m). Using the well-established polarization self-rotation effect in a room-temperature $^{87}$Rb vapor cell, we achieve -3 dB of vacuum squeezing within the noise spectral window of 100 Hz to several MHz, corresponding to 3.5 dB squeezing when accounting for optical losses. Leveraging this level of squeezing, we demonstrate a magnetic field sensitivity of approx. 1 pT$/$$\sqrt{Hz}$. The combination of vacuum-squeezed light and micrometer-scale vapor cells paves the way for compact, low-power-consumption atomic sensors with enhanced performance.</summary>
